What Is Career Intelligence? The Category the Job Search Industry Missed

By George West-Perry, Founder of Vetted: Career Intelligence

April 22, 2026

The job search industry has spent decades solving one problem: getting you to apply more effectively. Better resume keywords. Optimized cover letters. Interview coaching. ATS-beating formatting. LinkedIn profile optimization. Networking scripts.

Every tool, every coach, every platform is oriented toward the same moment: the application. Get more interviews. Convert more offers. Move faster through the funnel.

Nobody built anything for the decision that comes before all of that — whether to pursue a role at all.

That gap is what career intelligence fills.

A Definition

Career intelligence is the discipline of evaluating job opportunities against a structured, personalized framework before you invest time in applying, before you commit to a process, and before you accept an offer.

It's not gut feel. It's not advice from a career coach who doesn't know your priorities. It's not a salary comparison on Glassdoor. It's a systematic evaluation of every role against what actually matters to you — weighted by your own priorities, scored objectively, and returned as a clear signal: Pursue, Monitor, or Pass.

Career intelligence is the decision layer that sits above every other job search tool. You don't need to optimize your resume for a role you should be passing on. You don't need to prepare for an interview with a company that scores a Pass against your framework. Career intelligence filters first. Everything else follows.

Why This Category Didn't Exist Before

The job search industry's incentives are misaligned with the job seeker's actual needs. Job boards make money on volume — more postings, more applications, more traffic. Resume tools make money on anxiety — the belief that one more revision will unlock the opportunity. ATS optimizers make money on the arms race between job seekers and automated screening software.

None of these tools benefit from you applying less. None of them are designed to tell you "this role is a Pass — don't spend your time on it." The entire ecosystem is structured to generate activity, not to generate clarity.

Career intelligence is a fundamentally different orientation. It starts with the question: is this worth my time? And it answers that question systematically, before anything else happens.

The Five Dimensions of Career Intelligence

Career intelligence evaluates every opportunity across five dimensions:

Financial Accountability — not just compensation, but whether the role has a clear relationship between what you do and what you're paid, and whether it builds a quantifiable track record.

Access to Leadership — whether the role gives you meaningful visibility to decision-makers, not just a presence on an org chart.

Clear Success Measures — whether you can define what success looks like in concrete terms before you start, and whether the organization agrees.

Organizational Impact — whether the work is central to what the organization is trying to accomplish, or peripheral to it.

Role Integrity — whether the role matches what it said it was in the posting and the interview process.

Each dimension is weighted by the individual based on their current career priorities. The weighted total produces a VQ score — Vetted Quotient — that drives a Pursue, Monitor, or Pass recommendation.

Career Intelligence Is a Skill

Like any analytical discipline, career intelligence improves with practice. The first time you run your framework against a role, you're calibrating — learning how your weights translate to scores and whether your filters capture what actually matters to you. By the tenth evaluation, you're running a practiced process that takes minutes and produces genuinely useful signal.

The professionals who develop career intelligence as a skill don't just make better individual decisions. They develop a fundamentally different relationship with the job market. They spend less time on roles that don't fit. They invest more deeply in roles that do. They negotiate from a position of clarity rather than desperation. They say no more confidently — and yes more deliberately.
