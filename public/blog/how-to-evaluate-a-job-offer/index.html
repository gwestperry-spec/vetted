How to Evaluate a Job Offer: A Five-Filter Framework That Changes Everything

By George West-Perry, Founder of Vetted: Career Intelligence

April 22, 2026

Most people evaluate job offers the same way: they look at the salary, imagine themselves in the role for a few minutes, talk to a few people who tell them it sounds great, and either accept or walk away. Then they wonder why, six months later, it feels wrong.

The problem isn't the decision. It's the process — or more accurately, the complete absence of one.

After a decade working in senior roles across QSR, food manufacturing, and supply chain — and watching talented professionals take jobs they immediately knew were wrong — I built a framework to make this decision systematic. Not complicated. Systematic. Here's how it works.

Why Most Job Offer Evaluations Fail

The job search industry has optimized for one thing: getting you to apply faster. Better keywords. Stronger resume. A cover letter that beats the ATS. Nobody built anything to help you decide whether to apply at all — or whether to accept what came back.

The result is that most professionals make high-stakes career decisions using a combination of gut feel, financial pressure, and social proof from people who don't actually know what you need. That's not evaluation. That's a guess.

The framework I'll walk you through takes roughly fifteen minutes to run against any opportunity. It won't tell you what you want to hear. It will tell you what you need to know.

The Five Filters

Filter 1: Financial Accountability

This isn't just compensation — it's whether the role has a clear relationship between what you do and what you're paid. Can you demonstrate financial impact in this position? Is there a bonus structure tied to outcomes you control? Will this role put quantifiable results on your record that you can take to the next opportunity?

A high salary in a role with no financial visibility is a short-term win and a long-term trap. A lower salary with clear ownership over a budget or revenue line can compound into something much more valuable over three years.

Filter 2: Access to Leadership

Will you have meaningful visibility to decision-makers? Not a once-a-quarter all-hands. Actual access — the ability to present work, influence direction, and build relationships with people who determine what the organization does next.

Leadership access determines how fast you learn and how fast you grow. A role that keeps you several layers below any meaningful decision-making is a career in neutral regardless of the title.

Filter 3: Clear Success Measures

Can you describe, in concrete terms, what success looks like in this role at ninety days, six months, and one year? Does the hiring manager agree with that description? If you can't get a crisp answer to either question in the interview process, you won't get a crisp answer after you start.

Ambiguous success criteria is how good people end up in bad situations they couldn't have predicted. The company defines success after the fact, and it rarely matches what you thought you were hired to do.

Filter 4: Organizational Impact

Is this role central to what the organization is trying to accomplish, or is it peripheral? Will the work you do be seen, valued, and acted on? Or will it disappear into a process that nobody checks?

Impact isn't about ego — it's about whether you'll be able to point to something real when the next performance review or job search comes around. Roles that exist on the margins of organizational priorities are the first to be restructured and the last to be celebrated.

Filter 5: Role Integrity

Does this role match what it said it was in the job posting and the interview? Is the scope what you discussed? Are the reporting relationships what you were told? Are the resources actually available?

Role integrity is the filter that catches the bait-and-switch before you experience it. It's also the filter that reveals whether the organization knows what it's actually asking you to do — which tells you a great deal about what working there will be like.

How to Weight the Filters

The five filters are not equally important to everyone. A professional mid-career who is financially stable cares more about organizational impact and leadership access than they do about compensation. A professional returning to the workforce after a gap cares more about role integrity and clear success measures than anything else.

The framework only works when you weight it to your actual situation — not a generic "what matters in a job" list. Before you evaluate any specific opportunity, rank the five filters in order of personal priority. Then score each one on a scale for the role you're considering. The weighted total is your signal.

The Verdict System: Pursue, Monitor, or Pass

After running the five filters, you end up in one of three places:

Pursue: The role scores strongly against your priorities. Invest fully — optimize the application, prepare deeply, engage the process with real commitment.

Monitor: The role has genuine upside but meaningful gaps. Keep it in play, gather more information, and re-evaluate as you learn more. Don't rule it out, but don't go all-in yet.

Pass: The role scores poorly against what actually matters to you. Let it go — regardless of how good it looks on paper, regardless of the salary, regardless of who referred you. Your time in a job search is finite. Spend it on Pursue opportunities.

The Pass verdict is the most important one. Most people never consciously say no to a job they know is wrong. They apply, they interview, they get the offer, and then they either take it despite the reservations or they walk away feeling like they wasted everyone's time. The framework lets you make the Pass decision before any of that happens.
